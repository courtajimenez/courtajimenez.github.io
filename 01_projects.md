## Reinforcement Learning Constructs Shape Subjective Connection and Social Memory

**What it's about:** Modeling how reward, expected value, and prediction error — core reinforcement learning constructs — shape people's moment-to-moment sense of social connection and what they remember from social interactions.

<p class="project-meta">Courtney A. Jimenez &amp; Meghan L. Meyer · Department of Psychology, Columbia University · In Prep.</p>

**Approach:** Participants rated a target character's behavior for social reward across a continuous timecourse (using reality-TV video clips), then completed a social decision-making task and a surprise memory test. A reinforcement learning model (reward prediction error updating expected value, softmax choice rule) was fit to participants' friendship decisions in R.

**Results:**
- Friendship reward value, expected value, and prediction error all significantly predicted participants' subjective connection ratings
- Individual differences in fitted RL parameters (learning rate *α*, inverse temperature *β*) related to participants' real-world social experience
- Memory was better for moments of *lower* subjective connection and *higher* social reward value — suggesting affectively surprising or motivationally salient moments are prioritized in memory

## The Dorsomedial Prefrontal Cortex Prioritizes Social Learning During Rest

**What it's about:** Testing whether the brain preferentially consolidates social information during rest, using neural pattern reinstatement — directly relevant to how memory systems prioritize different types of information.

<p class="project-meta">Courtney Jimenez &amp; Meghan Meyer · Department of Psychology, Columbia University · <a href="https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2309232121">PNAS, 2024</a></p>

**Approach:** Participants encoded social and non-social stimuli, rested, and completed a memory test. High-dimensional fMRI pattern reinstatement during rest was measured in the dorsomedial prefrontal cortex (DMPFC, a social-cognition hub) and the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (VLPFC, implicated in non-social memory), and related to later memory performance across early, middle, and late rest periods.

**Results:**
- Social memory performance was positively correlated with DMPFC reinstatement during rest; non-social memory performance was positively correlated with VLPFC reinstatement
- Social consolidation in the DMPFC was specific to *early* rest, while non-social consolidation in the VLPFC showed no such early-rest specificity
- Findings support the DMPFC — a key default-network node — as prioritizing social information consolidation, consistent with the brain being "social by default"
