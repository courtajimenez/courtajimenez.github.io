## Reinforcement Learning Constructs Shape Subjective Connection and Social Memory from Moment-to-Moment Social Experience

**Courtney A. Jimenez & Meghan L. Meyer** · Department of Psychology, Columbia University · *In Prep.*

Our satisfaction with our relationships is one of the strongest predictors of subjective well-being, yet little is known about what drives our moment-to-moment sense of social connection, or what we remember from it. This project tests whether reinforcement learning (RL) constructs shape both our subjective experience of connection and our memory for social interactions.

Using video clips from reality television, participants rated a target character's behavior for social reward across a continuous timecourse, then completed a social decision-making task and a surprise memory test. An RL model (reward prediction error updating expected value, softmax choice) was fit to participants' friendship decisions.

**Key findings:**
- Friendship reward value, expected value, and prediction error all significantly predicted participants' subjective connection ratings
- Individual differences in RL parameters (learning rate *α*, inverse temperature *β*) related to participants' real-world social experience
- Memory was better for moments of *lower* subjective connection and *higher* social reward value — suggesting affectively surprising or motivationally salient moments are prioritized in memory

## The Dorsomedial Prefrontal Cortex Prioritizes Social Learning During Rest

**Courtney Jimenez & Meghan Meyer** · Department of Psychology, Columbia University · [PNAS, 2024.](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2309232121)

We encounter social and non-social information simultaneously in daily life, yet social memory is consistently better than memory for non-social information. This project tested whether the brain preferentially consolidates social information during rest, using neural pattern reinstatement in the dorsomedial prefrontal cortex (DMPFC) — a hub of social cognition — compared to the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (VLPFC), implicated in non-social memory.

Participants encoded social and non-social stimuli, rested, and then completed a memory test. Neural reinstatement of encoding patterns during rest was measured and related to later memory performance across early, middle, and late rest periods.

**Key findings:**
- Social memory performance was positively correlated with DMPFC reinstatement during rest; non-social memory performance was positively correlated with VLPFC reinstatement
- Social consolidation in the DMPFC was specific to *early* rest, while non-social consolidation in the VLPFC showed no such early-rest specificity
- Findings support the DMPFC — a key default network node — as prioritizing social information consolidation, consistent with the brain being "social by default"
